The Universal Functorial Heat Dominance Theorem: A Computable, Scale-Invariant Law of Stability for Non-Reversible Systems
Abstract
This work introduces and proves the Universal Functorial Heat Dominance Theorem (UFHDT), a mathematically sharp and empirically auditable law that quantifies stability in complex, non-reversible systems. The central challenge in analyzing such systems is controlling transient amplification—a phenomenon where perturbations can grow significantly before decaying—which is invisible to standard eigenvalue analysis. The UFHDT overcomes this by establishing a rigorous bound on the system's physical stability flow, a key measure of structural reinforcement versus novelty amplification. The theorem's main contributions are threefold: first, it provides explicit, computable formulas for a bounding constant map C and a calibrated admissible-time window [t??,t+?] within which the bound is sharp, transforming it into a predictive tool. Second, it is proven to be functorially invariant under Renormalization Group (RG) coarse-graining, establishing it as a scale-portable physical law applicable across different levels of system description. Third, it is bound to a complete measurement-and-validation contract, including a non-normal-safe estimator, audit protocols, and preregistered falsification criteria, ensuring its empirical integrity. By unifying stability analysis across discrete, continuous, and quantum settings, the UFHDT serves as a predictive, keystone component of Unified Predictive Assembly Theory (UPAT).
1. Introduction: The Problem of Stability in Irreversible Systems
1.1. Situating the Problem within Unified Predictive Assembly Theory (UPAT)
A central goal of modern science is to understand how stable, complex structures emerge and persist in systems operating far from thermodynamic equilibrium. Unified Predictive Assembly Theory (UPAT) provides a candidate first-principles framework for this problem, grounded in axioms of Thermodynamic Persistence and Geometric Invariance. Within this framework, a fundamental question remains: what are the dynamic laws that govern the stability of an assembly process over time? The UFHDT is proposed as the dynamic engine of UPAT, providing a concrete, predictive law that answers this question.   
This work reframes the concept of stability itself, moving beyond a static, long-term property to a dynamic, multi-stage lifecycle with predictable phases. Traditional stability analysis, such as Lyapunov theory, primarily asks whether a system will return to equilibrium after a perturbation. The UFHDT addresses a more nuanced and physically relevant set of questions for complex systems: How large can a perturbation grow before it decays? How long does this transient phase last? And how do these properties depend on the system's structure at different scales? This process-oriented view is essential for understanding systems whose function is intrinsically tied to their transient dynamics, such as cognitive, computational, or evolutionary processes, where the journey is as significant as the destination.   
1.2. The Analytical Cost of Irreversibility and Non-Normality
The primary analytical difficulty in modeling such systems arises from their inherent irreversibility. The dynamics of reversible systems are governed by self-adjoint (or normal) operators, whose real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors ensure that perturbations decay monotonically. The analysis is, in this sense, "analytically cheap". In contrast, the generators of non-reversible processes—from chemical reaction networks to recurrent neural networks—are non-normal operators, meaning the operator L does not commute with its adjoint (LL??=L?L).   
This non-normality has profound physical consequences. The non-orthogonality of eigenvectors permits constructive interference between modes, leading to transient amplification: the norm of the evolution semigroup, ?etL?, can grow substantially before its eventual asymptotic decay dictated by the eigenvalues. This transient growth corresponds to a physical phenomenon that can be termed "emergence friction"—the formation and subsequent decay of complex, intermediate states that are critical to the system's evolution but are not part of its final steady state. Because a purely eigenvalue-based analysis is blind to this behavior, a more powerful mathematical apparatus is required. The "analytical cost" of irreversibility must be paid by employing tools such as pseudospectral theory to control transient effects and generalized geometric inequalities to bound the asymptotic decay rate. This cost is not merely a mathematical abstraction; it reflects a physical reality. The apparent simplicity of a non-normal system's spectrum hides immense complexity in its transient dynamics, and the tools used in this paper are the necessary payment to uncover the system's true physical behavior.

from scipy.linalg import expm
from scipy.sparse.linalg import expm_multiply

def compute_beta_t(L, pi, t_points):
    """Computes the stability flow ?(t) via sparse semigroup action."""
    K_xx_t = np.zeros((len(t_points), L.shape))
    # Iterate through each starting state to compute the diagonal of the heat kernel
    for i in range(L.shape):
        p0 = np.zeros(L.shape)
        p0[i] = 1.0
        # Use efficient method for action of matrix exponential on a vector
        # Note: expm_multiply computes exp(A*t) @ B. Here L is for row vectors p' = pL,
        # so for column vectors p' = L^T p, we use L.T.
        pt_over_time = expm_multiply(L.T, p0, start=t_points, stop=t_points[-1], num=len(t_points))
        # Extract the return probability K(i,i,t) = (e^{tL})_{ii}
        K_xx_t[:, i] = pt_over_time[:, i]
    
    # Normalize K_xx_t element-wise by the stationary probability vector pi and compute expectation
    K_norm = (2 * K_xx_t - (pi + 1)) / (pi - 1)
    log_K_exp = np.sum(pi * np.log(K_norm + 1e-9), axis=1) # Use 1e-9 for epsilon
    
    # Compute derivative using central differences
    beta_t = np.gradient(log_K_exp, t_points)
    return beta_t
