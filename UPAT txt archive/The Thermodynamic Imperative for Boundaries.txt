This work provides a first-principles proof for the emergence of boundaries, such as cell membranes, in any persistent, far-from-equilibrium system. We show that the laws of thermodynamics, when applied to complex systems with long-range correlations, necessitate a specific information-theoretic potential (Tsallis relative entropy) to be minimized. The result provides a new, falsifiable basis for a predictive science of life by demonstrating how bounded structures are a necessary, not contingent, feature of our universe.

1. Introduction
1.1. The Ontological Problem of Boundaries in Theories of Complexity
A central challenge in the physical and biological sciences is to formulate a predictive theory of persistence—a first-principles account of how complex, adaptive systems emerge and maintain their integrity against the dissipative pressures of the universe. This challenge is fundamentally a question about the origin of boundaries. Before one can explain how a "thing" (a cell, an organism, a cognitive agent) perceives, acts, or learns, one must first explain the existence of the "thing" itself: the boundary that partitions it from the rest of the world.
Leading frameworks for complexity and cognition have struggled to provide a satisfactory, non-circular answer to this question. The Free Energy Principle (FEP), a powerful and influential paradigm, posits that any persistent system must act to minimize its variational free energy, an information-theoretic upper bound on surprise. However, the entire FEP formalism is predicated on a pre-existing statistical partition known as a Markov blanket, which separates a system's internal states from its external environment. This has led to trenchant critiques that the FEP assumes the very structure it seeks to explain, reifying the boundary as an axiom rather than deriving it as a consequence of physical law. This has been termed the "Markov blanket trick"—an instrumental modeling choice that is given undue ontological weight, leaving the physical mechanism of boundary formation and maintenance unresolved.
Concurrently, Assembly Theory (AT) attempts to quantify complexity by measuring the historical contingency of an object's construction via its "assembly index." While a useful tool for identifying the products of selection, AT has been critiqued as a static, combinatorial accounting system, formally equivalent to concepts in data compression, that lacks a forward-in-time, predictive dynamic for how systems persist as non-equilibrium steady states (NESS). Both frameworks, in different ways, highlight the absence of a physically-grounded, irreversible dynamic capable of explaining the emergence of stable, bounded structures from first principles.
1.2. Tsallis Entropy for Non-Equilibrium, Non-Extensive Systems
This work argues that a key reason for this theoretical gap has been an implicit reliance on an information-theoretic toolkit—namely, that based on Shannon entropy—that is fundamentally unsuited for the systems in question. Shannon entropy and the associated Boltzmann-Gibbs (BG) statistical mechanics are the foundational tools for describing extensive, ergodic systems at or near thermodynamic equilibrium, where correlations are local and short-range. Complex, persistent systems, however, are quintessentially non-equilibrium, non-extensive, and characterized by long-range correlations, multiscale organization, and power-law statistical distributions.
The correct physical tool for such systems is the generalized statistical mechanics introduced by Constantino Tsallis. Tsallis entropy, Sq?, is a one-parameter generalization of Shannon entropy that is explicitly non-additive. For two independent systems A and B, the total entropy is given by Sq?(A+B)=Sq?(A)+Sq?(B)+(1?q)Sq?(A)Sq?(B). This non-additivity, which vanishes in the Shannon limit (q?1), is not a mathematical curiosity but the essential feature required to describe systems with strong, long-range correlations. The choice of Tsallis entropy is therefore not a matter of convenience; it is a physical necessity dictated by the problem domain. Any successful theory of non-equilibrium persistence must generalize beyond the Shannon/BG framework. The failure of previous theories to derive boundary emergence can be partly attributed to their implicit reliance on an equilibrium-based information theory.

2. Minimal Axioms and the Tsallis Framework
2.1. The Thermodynamic Imperative for Dissipative Structures
The proof begins from the two most fundamental laws of physics.
Axiom I: Conservation of Energy (The First Law of Thermodynamics). Axiom II: Non-decreasing Total Entropy (The Second Law of Thermodynamics).
Together, these axioms dictate the universal drive of the cosmos towards thermodynamic equilibrium, a state of maximum entropy characterized by the absence of gradients. For a system to exist and persist in a state far from equilibrium, it cannot violate these laws. Instead, it must become a stable and efficient channel for enacting them. Such a system organizes itself into a dissipative structure—a complex, ordered state that is maintained by a continuous flow of energy and matter, which it harnesses to degrade environmental gradients (e.g., thermal or chemical) more efficiently than its disorganized surroundings. The imperative to maintain this structure against fluctuations, and thus to continue serving as an effective conduit for entropy production, is the ultimate driver of kinetic selection in non-equilibrium systems.
